Background

I grew up refurbishing computers. Dinosaur parts. Clanking hard drives. Machines that had been running for a decade, each one different from the last. My parents would bring them home in job lots—destined for internet cafes across the ocean in the Philippines, where our family could put them to use.

It took patience. You had to listen to the machine. Understand its quirks. That's where I learned that if you want something to last, you have to put in the work.

The Pivot

But by the time we were ready, the world had moved on. Smartphones replaced desktop culture. DOTA became Mobile Legends. YouTube became an app. The plan had to change.

So we pivoted: local ISP services in remote Philippine towns. Through optimized routing and thoughtful infrastructure, we proved that stable connectivity didn't require exceptional bandwidth—just better design. I helped build the proof of concept before family circumstances took me elsewhere, but the lesson stuck:

Technology is only as good as the problem it solves.

The Thread

It's all the same work. IT infrastructure, content, STEM—they're just different ways to solve the problem. The tech needs to work. The story needs to land. You can't separate them. The engineering needs a competitive edge. It's all interconnected.

Current Status

Right now, I'm at the University of Toronto studying Computer Science, exploring where LLMs, data centers, and economic uncertainty intersect. In unsettling times, there's opportunity—I want to understand how it appears. I'm also running a business in cabinetry with my brother, keeping my options open while I finish my degree.

When I'm sold on an idea, I read everything I can about it. I want to feel competent before I share my work publicly. Outside of tech, I'm into photography—film photography, specifically. My first camera, a Nikon D3200, was passed down by a good friend who also got me into film with a Minox point-and-shoot and a Nikon EM (apparently one of Nikon's worst models, which made me love it more). I'm looking into Super 8 and 16mm cinematography next.
